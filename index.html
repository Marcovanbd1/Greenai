A message to the Green Party

M.R.
Winterburn

The old world is ending.
The question is what fills the vacuum.

Read on

Not metaphorically.

The US/Israel war on Iran is not an isolated conflict. It is a signal. The post-war order — the one that shaped every political assumption your party was built inside — is cracking along fault lines that have been accumulating stress for thirty years.

What fills the vacuum matters enormously.

Right now the answer is: rhetoric. Corruption. Strongmen. The last gasps of a two-party adversarial system that was already a poor fit for the complexity of the world it was supposed to govern.

You can hear it creaking.

The Green Party is at a threshold.

This is not normal. This has never happened before.

The question is not whether the Greens are growing. They are. The question is whether the party is ready to convert that growth into something that changes the country — and possibly arrives at exactly the right moment to help shape what comes next.

That requires a different kind of thinking.

Hope was the right word.
For the moment it named.

But the Green Party is past hope now.

Hope is what you hold onto when you're outside. The Greens are no longer outside. They are arriving — at a moment of geopolitical instability, institutional collapse, and a public that is looking, perhaps for the first time seriously, for a different kind of politics.

The word for this moment is progress.

Not the managed, centrist, third-way progress that discredited itself in the nineties. Not technocratic optimism. Not growth for its own sake.

Progress as in: we know where we're going, we know how to get there, and we're taking you with us.

That requires infrastructure the Green Party does not yet have.

A tension the party hasn't fully named.

The new members are young. Digital. They have grown up inside systems that the party's current communication architecture was not built for. They are not waiting to be persuaded — they are waiting to be organised.

The active infrastructure — the committees, the working groups, the policy machinery — was built by people with deep political commitment and real experience. But a different relationship with technology. A different relationship with AI specifically.

This is not a criticism. It is a structural observation. And it is urgent.

Because AI is not coming. It is here. It is already reshaping healthcare, public services, democratic participation, the information environment that politics itself operates in, and the nature of warfare — as Iran is demonstrating in real time.

The Green Party needs to decide what it thinks. Not in a vague, concerned way. Seriously. Philosophically. Practically.

Our first responsibility is to educate ourselves.

Not to adopt AI uncritically. Not to reject it reflexively. To understand it — what it actually does, what it cannot do, what it makes possible and what it forecloses.

I work with large language models daily. I lead digital transformation for an NHS Primary Care Network serving 77,000 patients in Dorset. I have watched what happens when technology meets human institutions under pressure — when the gap between what a system knows and what a person feels becomes the thing that determines whether care lands or fails.

The missing layer isn't more content, more policy, more evidence. It's understanding the emotional climate someone is arriving in before you try to move them. I call this Emotional Architecture. Not manipulation — the opposite. An act of respect that most political communication withholds. The Greens, more than any party, have the values base to do this authentically. But values without infrastructure plateaus.

You can train a hundred canvassers.
You cannot train 216,000 members.

Unless you build tools.

Not chatbots. Not automated content. Not any of the shallow applications that discredit the technology. Genuine infrastructure — tools that help the party understand the emotional landscape of specific communities. Tools that help candidates frame policy positions for specific human contexts. Tools that give canvassers real-time support grounded in actual local knowledge.

Nobody in British politics is doing this. Not seriously.

Not with the philosophical depth, psychological grounding, and technical capability to do it well.

That is the white space.

I have been thinking about Game Theory.

It sounds impossible — using strategic decision modelling to get the Greens into power. But so was the four minute mile. So were most things that changed the shape of what was thinkable.

The two-party system is not a law of nature. It is a Nash equilibrium that holds until the conditions that sustain it shift. Those conditions are shifting — faster than most people inside the system have noticed.

The Green Party, if it moves with intellectual rigour and strategic precision at exactly this moment, is not just a protest movement with good values. It is a serious political force arriving at the right time.

But only if it thinks like one.

The Green Party needs a Head of AI.

Not a digital communications manager. Not a tech advisor.

The role
Head of AI
  • Develop Green AI policy from first principles — not borrowed from think tanks, not reactive to Labour or the Tories. Original, grounded, philosophically coherent.
  • Build internal tools that make the party smarter and faster without losing its grassroots character.
  • Bridge the generational gap between new members and experienced organisers.
  • Position the Greens as the only political force in Britain thinking seriously about AI governance from a genuinely human-centred perspective.

No other party has this. No other party could do it authentically.

The Greens can.

Not just for the well-meaning
and the climate conscious.

For the thinkers. The introverts. The people who have been watching the old system fail and waiting — not with hope exactly, but with a kind of patient readiness — for something serious to arrive.

That party is possible.

The question is whether this is the moment someone decides to build it properly.
M.R. Winterburn — Isle of Portland, Dorset — 2026
About

M.R. Winterburn has spent thirty years inside institutions at the moment they had to change — television broadcast, the NHS, and the particular pressure of a small island community learning to think differently about itself.

He is a member of the Green Party.
